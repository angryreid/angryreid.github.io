Question:

I live in Colombia and I am coming to the end of my degree in economics. I am planning to study further in France, but I am not sure what I will do after that. How do I go about evaluating what kind of job I would like and to which I would be well suited? Male, 20s
(Jonathan Black is director of the Careers Service at the University of Oxford. He answers your questions on personal and career development and working life. )

Jonathanâ€™s answer

In Aliceâ€™s Adventures in Wonderland by Lewis Carroll, the Cheshire Cat told Alice that if she did not care where she wanted to go, it did not matter too much which direction she took â€” she was sure to get somewhere as long as she walked for long enough.
You probably do care, so before working through the process of generating and refining specific career choices, helicopter up to 10,000 feet from where you can view your overall career map, answer the big questions and set your overall direction.
Where, roughly, are you going? What is your aim? How long have you got? Remember that there are no right answers, only the best choice for you, based on what you know now.
Having laid out your overall map, set boundaries and defined your personal targets you can then turn to practicalities. Before you arrive in France and begin your studies, identify what you are good at, based on your skills and experiences.
Write down examples of how you demonstrate key employability skills such as team work, leadership, and communication.
You are looking for a potential match to what might suit you now, at this stage in your career; remember that this changes over time.
Your further studies will give you a deeper love of the subject, help you gain a higher qualification, and experience studying in France. In any event, you have a year to explore options.
Your career project might take some time, as at this stage you will probably be short of information. Go out and meet people, attend talks, go to relevant lectures, watch videos about industries and organisations, seek out websites that can give you impartial and general careers advice (for example, the open access Oxford university careers website).
Over time you can start to discard choices and then choose one. The evidence is that the more focused you are, the more likely you are to achieve your goal.
In 1804, Meriwether Lewis and William Clark set off with a compass and direction, and a general destination of mapping the new Louisiana territories in America. But they had no exact map; their successful expedition took two years, your career will be much longer but, like Lewis and Clark, it is about direction not destination.
